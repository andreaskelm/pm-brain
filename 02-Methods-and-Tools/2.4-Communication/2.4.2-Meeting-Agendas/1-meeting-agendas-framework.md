# Meeting Agendas Framework

## Overview

This framework helps product teams run effective meetings that drive decisions and action. Meetings are NOT bureaucracyâ€”they're strategic communication tools that enable alignment and progress.

## Core Philosophy

### Good Communication is NOT Optional

Effective meetings should:

- **Drive decisions over discussions** - Move things forward, not in circles
- **Respect time over tradition** - Every minute must add value
- **Enable clarity over comprehension** - Make understanding effortless
- **Foster action over information** - Create clear next steps

### Understanding Meetings

**MEETINGS (Synchronous Communication)**

- Real-time collaboration and decision-making
- Build alignment and shared understanding
- Resolve complex issues through dialogue
- Time-sensitive and focused

-----

## Step 0: Braindump & Think First (Critical!)

**Before scheduling any meeting, braindump:**
- What are you trying to accomplish? Dump everything - don't structure yet.
- What does your product sense tell you? Is a meeting really needed?
- What assumptions are you making? Could this be async instead?
- What biases might affect your meeting planning? (Meeting for meeting's sake? Status update trap?)

**Product sense exercise:**
- If you had to accomplish this in 15 minutes, how would you do it?
- What would make you say "this meeting is obviously unnecessary"?
- What would make you say "this meeting is obviously valuable"?

## ğŸ¯ Meeting Types & When to Use

### 1. Daily Standup (15 minutes)

**Purpose:** Synchronize team, surface blockers

**When to use:**

- Daily during active development
- Team needs quick alignment
- Working on interconnected work

**When NOT to use:**

- Team has no dependencies
- Async updates sufficient
- Different time zones make it painful

**Attendees:** Development team (required), Scrum Master (facilitates), Product Owner (optional)

-----

### 2. Sprint Planning (2-4 hours per 2-week sprint)

**Purpose:** Define sprint goal and commit to work

**When to use:**

- Beginning of each sprint
- Need team commitment
- Prioritizing backlog items

**When NOT to use:**

- Backlog isnâ€™t refined
- Key people unavailable
- Team capacity unknown

**Attendees:** Product Owner (required), Scrum Master (facilitates), Development Team (required)

-----

### 3. Sprint Review (1-2 hours per 2-week sprint)

**Purpose:** Demo work, gather feedback

**When to use:**

- End of sprint
- Have working software to show
- Need stakeholder input

**When NOT to use:**

- Nothing completed
- No stakeholders available
- Feature not ready for feedback

**Attendees:** Scrum Team (required), Stakeholders (invited), Customers (optional)

-----

### 4. Sprint Retrospective (1-1.5 hours per 2-week sprint)

**Purpose:** Reflect and improve process

**When to use:**

- End of every sprint
- Team needs to air concerns
- Process improvements needed

**When NOT to use:**

- Team morale too low (address first)
- Same issues never get fixed (escalate)
- Feels like checkbox exercise (redesign)

**Attendees:** Development Team (required), Scrum Master (facilitates), Product Owner (optional for first 30 min)

-----

### 5. Backlog Refinement (1-2 hours per week)

**Purpose:** Clarify, estimate, split stories

**When to use:**

- Weekly or mid-sprint
- Upcoming work needs detail
- Estimates are missing

**When NOT to use:**

- Backlog is well-maintained
- Team doing this elsewhere
- Turning into planning

**Attendees:** Product Owner (required), Development Team (required), Scrum Master (optional)

-----

### 6. 1-on-1s (30-60 minutes weekly/bi-weekly)

**Purpose:** Career growth, feedback, coaching

**When to use:**

- Regularly scheduled (weekly/bi-weekly)
- Relationship building
- Private feedback needed

**When NOT to use:**

- Only for status updates (use standup)
- Emergency issues (address immediately)
- Group topics (use team meeting)

**Attendees:** Manager + Direct Report

-----

### 7. Strategy/Planning Sessions (2-4 hours)

**Purpose:** Quarterly planning, roadmapping

**When to use:**

- Start of quarter
- Major strategic decisions
- Roadmap alignment needed

**When NOT to use:**

- Tactical decisions
- Frequent changes needed
- Havenâ€™t done homework

**Attendees:** Product Leadership, Key Stakeholders, Team Leads

### Anti-Pattern #2: Status Update Meetings

**What it looks like:**

- Everyone reports what they did
- No discussion or decisions

**Why itâ€™s harmful:**

- Doesnâ€™t require synchronous time

**Solution:**

- If someone needs clarity, they can ask questions async
- Save meetings for complex topics

-----

### Anti-Pattern #3: The Never-Ending Meeting

**What it looks like:**

- Consistently runs over time
- No clear endpoint
- People have to leave for next meeting

**Why itâ€™s harmful:**

- Disrespects peopleâ€™s time
- Creates cascade of late meetings
- Important items at end get rushed

**Solution:**

- Strict timeboxing with visible timer
- Agenda with time allocations
- Park off-topic discussions
- End 5 min early to allow transitions

### Anti-Pattern #4: No Decisions Made

**What it looks like:**

- Lots of discussion, no conclusions
- Same topics every meeting

**Why itâ€™s harmful:**

- Progress stalls

**Solution:**

- Clarify decision-making process upfront (consensus? majority? leader decides?)
- End each topic with explicit decision or next step
- Document decisions immediately
- Assign owners to actions

**What it looks like:**

- Key decision-makers absent
- Too many â€œFYIâ€ attendees

**Why itâ€™s harmful:**
